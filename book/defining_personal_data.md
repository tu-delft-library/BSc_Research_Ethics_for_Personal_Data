# I. Defining Personal Data 

## Defining Personal Data 	 

Researchers who collect personal data from people must adhere to the General Data Protection Regulation (**GDPR**), or *Algemene verordening gegevensbescherming* (*AVG*), which establishes legal guidelines for working with human research data within the European Union. We will not delve into the details of the GDPR in this mini-module, but the explanations and recommendations shared within these materials are in accordance with the law. 
<br>

:::{card} Definition of PERSONAL DATA:
:class-card: text-center
Any information that can be traced back to a unique person
:::
<br>

These pieces of information are personal data because they can be **directly** or **indirectly** traced back to a single person. Keep in mind that supporting documents or administrative artefacts used to organise a study can also contain personal data. For example, a contact list with the emails of research participants is considered personal data, as are copies of forms that research participants sign to give their consent to participate in the study (informed consent forms). 
<br>

## Ethical Questions   

As a researcher, it is your legal and ethical imperative to protect the personal data of the people participating in your study. When you design your study, you need to be very clear about what type(s) of personal data will be involved. Therefore, you must start by asking yourself the following questions: 
<br>

:::{card} Question #1
:class-card: text-center
What's my **research question** or **goal**?
:::

:::{card} Question #2
:class-card: text-center
Which data do I **need to collect** to answer my **research question**?
:::

:::{card} Question #3
:class-card: text-center
Are any of the data that I plan to collect **personal data** (i.e. can be linked to a specific person)? If yes, is each type of personal data **absolutely necessary** to answer **my research question**? 
:::

:::{card} Question #4
:class-card: text-center
Are the personal data <u>**directly**</u> or <u>**indirectly identifiable**</u>?
(We'll define these terms next.)   
:::
 
## Direct Identifiers
**Direct identifiers** (also called **directly identifiable personal information**) are pieces of personal data that are unique to an individual. A direct identifier standing alone is enough to directly identify an individual research participant. If a direct identifier is accidentally leaked, **the risk of exposing a participant’s identity and private information is high**. Therefore, direct identifiers should be treated with caution. ## Indirect Identifiers
**Indirect identifiers** (**indirectly identifiable personal information**) are data that could indirectly reveal a research participant's identity if combined with other available details. These additional details could be found either within the same dataset or separately, like on the web. Indirect identifiers can lead to reidentification in combination with other data. They are also referred to as **"quasi-identifiers”**. Like an indirect identifier, a quasi-identifier is a piece of data that standing alone won't identify a single person, but it could become identifying if other quasi-identifiers are available. It may seem like a dataset that has information about ages or shoe sizes wouldn't carry much risk. However, **indirectly identifiable personal information can still be risky** because the data can still be linked back to a specific individual. For example, it could be possible to re-identify study participants if indirect identifiers were combined with other details such as gender, workplace, or location, especially in research studies involving small groups of participants. 
<br>

## Grey Area: Indirect or Direct?  

In certain cases, the **context** will determine if data is directly versus indirectly identifiable. Let's look at two examples:  

```{admonition} Example 1: Grey Area for direct vs. indirect identifiers 
:class: tip
Broad information about geolocations (e.g. participants’ temporary locations such as a city, neighbourhood, or campus) can act as indirect identifiers in combination with other data points. However, in some cases the coordinates pinpoint a research participant's home address or specifically track a person's regular movements. In such situations, the geolocation standing alone could potentially be enough to directly reidentify a participant, so the geolocation data would be considered directly identifiable personal information.
```  

```{admonition} Example 2: Grey Area for direct vs. indirect identifiers
:class: tip
General information about gender or height can act as indirectly identifiable personal information: it might trace back to an individual person when combined with other pieces of data such as age or workplace. If the sample size is small enough, sometimes these details become much more directly identifiable. Imagine: all the employees of a small company take a survey about their satisfaction with changes implemented by their manager. The survey also asks them to indicate their gender. There is only one male staff member, so in this context, gender standing alone makes it possible to pinpoint his survey responses.  
```  
 
```{admonition} Be aware!
:class: warning
Whether the data is directly or indirectly identifiable personal information, you must implement measures to protect people's personal information: **all personal data must be processed and stored with care**.    
```

This video gives an example of a researcher who is delving into ethical questions as she plans and designs a study.

**Part I:**   

In this video, we will see a scenario of a researcher who will use ethical questions to guide the design of a study that involves personal data.  

This is Paula. She is designing a research project, and as an ethical researcher, she wants to prioritize the safety and privacy of her human research participants. Her goal is to conduct research ethically, or in a decent way, by protecting the privacy, safety, and wellbeing of her research participants.   

Therefore, starting in the planning phase of her study, Paula must ask herself a series of questions related to personal data.   

**What is the research question and/or goal?**  

Paula wants to know how well students in different academic programs can attend lectures based on the scheduled lecture time slots and locations. She plans to investigate whether there are barriers that hinder students from participating in this important aspect of learning during their academic experience. She wants to investigate if there are differences between academic programs, so she intends to collect opinions from students enrolled in four academic programs.   

**What data is necessary to answer the research question?**   

Paula will ask students to respond to a digital questionnaire. She determines that she will need to collect five data points to meet her research goals. Her digital questionnaire will ask students about:    

- Their academic programme  

- Their year of study  

- Their level of satisfaction with the accessibility of schedules (as measured on a five-point scale).   

- Whether they have requested schedule adjustments. If so, the reason for these schedule adjustments and whether their requests were fulfilled.   

- Any other existing barriers, in the student's opinion.    

Now that she's clear on what data is needed, Paula needs to ask herself several follow-up questions:   

**Will my project involve personal data? If yes, are the personal data absolutely necessary to answer my research question?**   

**Will I be collecting directly or indirectly identifiable personal information?**  

Indeed, Paula plans to collect personal data from her study participants. While she is mapping out her project, Paula also asks herself if she should also collect the names and student ID numbers of the students. But she determines that names and ID numbers are not essential to answer her research question or to organise her project. Paula decides that her questionnaire will not ask students about these personally identifiable details.  

Paula knows that the information about academic programme, year of study, and reasons for scheduling adjustments are personal data because these details can be traced back to individual respondents. Paula determines that these are examples of indirectly identifiable personal data: a student's academic program or year would not be enough to reidentify that student, but re-identification might be possible in combination with the reasons for scheduling changes.  

So before she starts collecting any data, Paula will need to carefully assess elements of potential risk in her project and identify ways to minimize or prevent them.  

This way she can prioritize the safety and privacy of her participants.
